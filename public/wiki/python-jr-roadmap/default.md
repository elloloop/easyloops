## Our Learning Rules

Before you start, here are four important rules for how we learn:

### 1. Always Type the Code With Your Own Hands

Never copy and paste code from these pages. Always type every letter yourself, even if it feels slow. This is like learning to ride a bike -- you cannot learn it just by watching someone else ride. Your fingers need to practice typing the code so your brain learns the patterns. Reading code is not the same as writing code.

### 2. While Loops First

When you get to the part about repeating things (loops), you will learn `while` loops first. A `while` loop is the simple, step-by-step way to repeat something. Think of it like counting on your fingers: you start at 1, check if you have reached 10, and keep going if you have not. Later, you will learn `for` loops, which are like a shortcut for the same thing. But you need to understand the long way before you learn the shortcut!

### 3. Labels on Everything (Type Hints)

When you start writing your own instructions for the computer (called functions), you will always put labels on them that say what kind of information goes in and what kind comes out. It is like labeling boxes in your room: "TOYS," "BOOKS," "ART SUPPLIES." The labels help you (and the computer) know what belongs where. In Python, these labels are called **type hints**, and they look like this: `def add(a: int, b: int) -> int:` -- this says "this instruction takes in two whole numbers and gives back a whole number."

### 4. Try It Right Away

After you read about something new, open your computer and try it immediately. Change things around. Break things on purpose! See what happens when you do something wrong. Your computer is your laboratory, and experiments are how you learn.

This is where it all begins. You will learn what a program is, what kinds of information exist, how to store things, do math, make decisions, and repeat actions.

### 2. Values and Types -- Numbers, Words, and Yes/No

**What you will learn:** There are different kinds of information: whole numbers (like 5), numbers with decimals (like 3.14), text (like "hello"), and yes/no answers (True/False). You will learn how to tell them apart.

**Why it matters:** The computer treats numbers and words very differently. Knowing which is which is like knowing the difference between ingredients in a kitchen -- you would not put salt where sugar goes!

**Go to:** [[wiki:python-jr-values-and-types]]

### 3. Variables -- Giving Names to Things

**What you will learn:** How to save information by giving it a name, so you can use it later. Like putting a label on a jar so you remember what is inside.

**Why it matters:** Without names for things, your programs cannot remember anything. Variables let your programs save and reuse information.

**Go to:** [[wiki:python-jr-variables-and-memory]]

### 4. Operators -- Doing Math and Comparing Things

**What you will learn:** How to add, subtract, multiply, and divide. How to compare things (Is 5 bigger than 3? Are these two things the same?). How to combine yes/no answers.

**Why it matters:** This is how your programs figure things out. Every calculator, every game, every app uses these building blocks.

**Go to:** [[wiki:python-jr-operators]]

### 5. Conditions -- Making Decisions

**What you will learn:** How to make your program choose what to do based on a situation. "If it is raining, bring an umbrella. Otherwise, wear sunglasses." Your programs will be able to make decisions just like that.

**Why it matters:** A program that cannot make decisions is just a list of steps that always does the same thing. Conditions make your programs smart!

**Go to:** [[wiki:python-jr-conditions]]

### 6. Loops -- Doing Things Over and Over

**What you will learn:** How to make the computer repeat something many times without you having to type it over and over. First you will learn the `while` loop (the careful, step-by-step way) and then later the `for` loop (the shortcut way).

**Why it matters:** Imagine writing "Happy Birthday" on 100 cards by hand versus telling a printer to do it. Loops give your programs the power to do millions of things in seconds.

**Go to:** [[wiki:python-jr-loops]]

## Study Tips

1. **Always type the code with your own hands.** Every single example in these lessons is meant to be typed by you, character by character. Do not copy and paste. Reading code is like reading about swimming -- it does not teach you to swim. Your fingers need to practice so your brain learns the patterns.

2. **Learn the long way first, then the shortcuts.** When you reach loops, use `while` loops for everything until repeating things feels natural. Only then learn `for` loops as a shortcut. You should be able to explain what any `for` loop is really doing step by step.

3. **Put labels on every function you write.** From your very first function, write labels (type hints) that say what kind of information goes in and what kind comes out. It is like labeling the ingredients for a recipe: "1 cup of FLOUR, 2 EGGS." The labels help you think clearly about what your code does before you write it.

4. **Try it right away.** After reading about something new, open your computer and try it immediately. Change the examples. Break things on purpose. See what error messages look like. The computer is your science lab.

5. **Do not move on until you understand.** If you get questions wrong, go back and study that topic again. Moving on with gaps is like building a tower on a wobbly base -- eventually it falls over.

6. **Do not skip the building phase (Phase 6).** It might be tempting to jump from learning Python to building internet apps. But building data structures yourself teaches you how things really work. It is the difference between someone who follows recipes and a chef who understands cooking.

7. **Build your own projects between phases.** After Phase 3, build a little tool that runs in the terminal. After Phase 5, build a text game. After Phase 9, build a full website. The lessons teach you ideas; projects teach you how to put them together.

8. **Read error messages carefully.** When your code breaks, Python tells you what went wrong. Read the whole message. It is not yelling at you -- it is giving you clues, like a treasure map to the problem.

9. **See the connections.** Every topic reuses ideas from earlier topics. Functions return values (Phase 1). Methods are functions attached to classes (Phase 4). If you notice how ideas connect, you truly understand programming.

## Answers to Practice Questions

**1.** `print()` tells the computer to show something on the screen. Whatever you put inside the parentheses gets displayed.

**2.** `42` (without quotes) is a number -- you can do math with it. `"42"` (with quotes) is text -- it is just the characters "4" and "2" written down. You cannot do math with text.

**3.** `score` is now 25. When you write `score = 25`, the old value (10) is replaced with the new value (25).

**4.** The first line shows `8` (because Python calculates 5 + 3). The second line shows `5 + 3` (because the quotes make it text, so Python just displays the characters as they are).

**5.** The code checks if `temperature` (which is 35) is greater than 30. Since 35 is greater than 30, it shows "It is hot!" on the screen.

**6.** A `while` loop is the step-by-step way to repeat something. You control everything: where to start, when to stop, and how to count. A `for` loop is a shortcut that does the counting for you automatically. They both repeat things, but `while` is more hands-on.

**7.** `fruits[0]` gives you `"apple"`. In Python, counting starts at 0, not 1. So the first item is at position 0.

**8.** A list is an ordered collection where you look things up by their position number (index). A dictionary is a collection where you look things up by a name (key). A list is like a numbered list; a dictionary is like a phone book.

**9.** `phone_book["Mom"]` gives you `"555-1234"`.

**10.** `len("hello")` gives you `5`, because the word "hello" has 5 characters (h-e-l-l-o).

**11.** No. Tuples cannot be changed after they are created. That is the whole point of a tuple -- it is frozen.

**12.** A list can have duplicate items and keeps things in order. A set only keeps unique items (no duplicates) and does not guarantee any particular order.

**13.** A function is a reusable set of instructions with a name. It is like a recipe: you write the recipe once, and then you can follow it anytime you want to make that dish. You do not rewrite the recipe every time.

**14.** The program crashes with an error message (`ZeroDivisionError`). Without error handling, the whole program stops running.

**15.** Variables only exist while the program is running. When the program stops, the information disappears. Files save information on your computer so it is still there when you run the program again later (or even after you restart your computer).

**16.**
```python
def greet(name: str) -> None:
    print("Hello, " + name + "!")
```

**17.** A module is a file full of code that someone else (or you) already wrote. You can bring it into your program and use it. For example, you might use a module that knows how to pick random numbers, so you do not have to figure out how to do that yourself.

**18.** A class is like a cookie cutter -- it defines the shape. An object is like a cookie -- it is the actual thing you made using the cookie cutter. You can use one cookie cutter to make many cookies, just like you can use one class to make many objects.

**19.** The class is `Dog` -- it is the blueprint that says "every dog has a name and a breed." An object is a specific dog you create from that blueprint, like a dog named "Buddy" who is a "Golden Retriever."

**20.** To prevent other parts of your program from accidentally changing important information. Like how a vending machine hides the money inside -- you can put money in and get snacks out, but you cannot reach in and grab the cash.

**21.** A "Sports Car" inherits from "Car." It has everything a car has (wheels, engine, steering wheel) plus extra features (spoiler, turbo engine). Or: a "Puppy" inherits from "Dog" -- it has everything a dog has, plus some puppy-specific things.

**22.** Abstraction means hiding the complicated details and only showing the simple parts that matter. Like using a TV remote -- you press a button to change the channel without needing to understand the electronics inside.

**23.** They produce the same result, but a comprehension does it in one line instead of several. A comprehension is a shortcut. Both are fine to use; the comprehension is just more compact.

**24.** A decorator wraps a function inside another function to add extra abilities. It is like putting a phone case on your phone -- the phone still works the same way, but now it has extra features (like protection or a kickstand).

**25.** Type hints help you think about what kind of information your code works with. They help you catch mistakes before running the code. They also help other people (and your future self) understand your code more quickly.

**26.** A stack is like a pile of plates: you put a plate on top and take a plate off the top (last in, first out). A queue is like a line at the lunch counter: the first person in line gets served first (first in, first out).

**27.** Sorting puts things in order, which makes them easier to find and work with. In everyday life: books in a library are sorted by author name, contacts in your phone are sorted alphabetically, and search results are sorted by relevance.

**28.** When a program is "on the internet," it is running on a computer (called a server) that other computers can talk to over the internet. Someone on another computer sends a request (like typing a website address), and the server sends back a response (like the webpage). You need to put your program on a server and make sure it can receive and respond to these requests.
